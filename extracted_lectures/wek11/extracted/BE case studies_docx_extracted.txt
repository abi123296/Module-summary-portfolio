Quarter-End Adjustments at Serendib Manufacturing (Pvt) Ltd
Context: Serendib Manufacturing is a listed company in Sri Lanka producing industrial components. The company has just secured a large foreign investor. Quarterly earnings are expected to show a 12% growth.
Two weeks before reporting:
The CFO realizes that raw material wastage has increased due to machine inefficiencies.
Recognizing the wastage will reduce profits by 8%.
The CEO tells the Finance Manager:
“Let’s classify part of this as capital improvement expense. It will smooth the numbers. Next quarter will compensate.”
Additionally:
The external audit firm has provided tax consultancy services to the company for the past 3 years.
The CFO’s bonus is linked to EBITDA.
The Finance Manager is on probation.
Group Task:
Section A – Identifying Issues
Which CIMA Code of Ethics principles are at risk in this scenario? Explain why.
Identify the ethical threats present (e.g., self-interest, intimidation, self-review). How do they appear?
What could be the short-term and long-term consequences if the financial adjustments are manipulated?
Section B – Evaluating Options
What safeguards or actions could the Finance Manager take to maintain integrity while addressing CEO pressure?
Should the Finance Manager follow the CEO’s instructions to capitalize expenses? Why or why not?
How could the external auditor’s role affect the ethical decision-making process?
Section C – Reflection
How can professional competence and due care be maintained under pressure?
If you were in the Finance Manager’s position, what would be your ethical course of action?
What lessons can be learned for handling similar financial reporting dilemmas in Sri Lanka?
Digital Lending and Data Transparency
A Sri Lankan fintech company offering Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL) services uses AI-based credit scoring.
Customers are not informed:
That behavioral data from social media is analyzed.
That algorithmic bias may disadvantage certain districts.
That late fee structures are dynamic and personalized.
The Head of Risk knows:
The algorithm unfairly downgrades applicants from certain income clusters.
The board argues:
“This is how we manage default risk. It’s commercially necessary.”
A management accountant in the firm must sign off on risk disclosure statements.
Group Task:
Section A – Identifying Issues
Which CIMA ethical principles are at risk in this case? Explain how each is affected.
Identify the ethical threats present (e.g., integrity threat, advocacy threat, self-interest).
What are the potential consequences (legal, reputational, operational) of not disclosing algorithmic bias to customers?
Section B – Evaluating Options
Should the management accountant disclose algorithmic bias to regulators or customers? What are the advantages and disadvantages?
What safeguards could be applied to mitigate ethical risks in AI-driven credit scoring?
How can the company balance transparency with competitive advantage?
Section C – Reflection
How would you ensure professional behavior and objectivity in digital financial services?
If you were an internal auditor, how would you handle this situation?
What measures could fintech companies in Sri Lanka adopt to improve trust and transparency with customers?
Family Ties in Procurement
A large construction firm in Colombo is bidding for a government infrastructure contract.
The Procurement Manager:
Is related to the Director of one of the bidding subcontractors.
Did not formally disclose this relationship.
Claims he can “remain objective.”
The subcontractor’s bid is not the lowest but promises political “stability.”
The company’s board pressures:
“Choose the safest political option. We cannot risk future tenders.”
1. Identify Ethical Issues
Which CIMA Code of Ethics principles are at risk?
What ethical threats (familiarity, advocacy, intimidation, self-interest, self-review) can you identify?
What are the possible consequences if the conflict is ignored?
2. Evaluate Possible Actions
What steps could the Procurement Manager take to handle the conflict of interest?
Should the relationship with the subcontractor be disclosed formally to the board? Why or why not?
How could the Procurement Manager respond to board pressure while maintaining integrity? List at least two alternatives.
3. Reflect and Recommend
How can professional behavior and integrity be maintained without harming the company’s business interests?
If you were an internal auditor, how would you report this situation?
Provide your recommended course of action and justify it using the CIMA ethical framework.
