Slide 36: 
Content: Handshake etiquette | A handshake may be appropriate in many professional contexts, but it is not compulsory in every culture or situation. Guidelines: Do not force physical contact. Wait for the other person’s cue if unsure. Keep handshake brief and confident. Avoid crushing grip or limp hand. If someone does not shake hands for cultural, religious, health, or personal reasons, respect it. A nod, smile, or “Good morning” is also professional.

Slide 41: 
Content: Respecting Personal Boundaries | Boundaries are invisible lines that protect comfort, privacy, and dignity. Types of boundaries Physical boundaries: personal space, touch, photos, handshakes. Emotional boundaries: not forcing someone to share personal issues. Digital boundaries: not adding people to groups without need, not messaging late at night unnecessarily. Professional boundaries: not becoming overly familiar with teachers, recruiters, or colleagues. Conversation boundaries: avoiding sensitive topics unless relevant and welcome.

Slide 42: 
Content: Respecting Personal Boundaries | Examples of boundary violations Touching someone’s hair, clothes, hijab, turban, or accessories without permission. Taking photos or videos without consent. Commenting on body weight, skin, complexion, height, or attractiveness. Asking classmates about salary, family income, relationship status, religion, or caste. Messaging a professor casually at midnight: “Hi sir, reply fast.” Standing too close in a queue or conversation. Pressuring someone to join a party, group, or activity.

Slide 43: 
Content: Respecting Personal Boundaries | Boundary-respecting phrases “Is this a good time to discuss the assignment?” “May I take a photo of the group?” “Would you be comfortable presenting the first part?” “I respect your view. I see it differently.” “I prefer not to discuss that, but I’m happy to talk about the project.” | If you accidentally cross a boundary Use a simple repair: “I’m sorry. I didn’t realize that made you uncomfortable. I won’t repeat it.” Do not over-explain. Do not say: “You are too sensitive.” That makes the mistake worse.
