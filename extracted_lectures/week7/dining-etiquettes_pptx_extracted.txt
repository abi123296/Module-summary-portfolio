Slide 6: 
Content: Ordering with Confidence | 01 | Scan the full menu | Read all sections — appetizers, mains, sides, desserts — before deciding. Know the price range so your order is appropriate for the occasion. | 02 | Don't order first | Let the host or eldest person order first. This also gives you time to gauge portion sizes and the formality of the meal. | 03 | Speak clearly to the server | Say 'I'd like...' not 'Gimme...' Use please and thank you. Make eye contact. Close your menu when ready to signal you've decided. | 04 | Match course count | If at a formal dinner, order the same number of courses as others. Don't be the only one who skips a course — it rushes others. | 05 | Dietary needs — handle privately | Alert the server early and quietly about allergies or dietary restrictions. Avoid making it a topic at the table unless asked. | 06 | Don't negotiate the menu | Avoid making excessive substitutions or asking for 'off-menu' items unless you have a real need. Keep special requests to a minimum.

Slide 7: 
Content: Posture, Body Language & Personal Space | Sit up straight | Back slightly away from the chair back. No slumping. Both feet flat on the floor. | 💪 | Elbows off the table | Wrists or forearms may rest on the table between courses, but not elbows while eating. | Phone goes away | In 2025, phone use at the table (even under it) is widely considered disrespectful. Face down or in your bag. | Eye contact & engagement | Look at the person speaking. Nod and respond. Staring at your plate signals disinterest. | Chew with mouth closed | Always. No exceptions. Take smaller bites to make conversation easier during meals. | 🛑 | No reaching across | Ask someone to pass the dish. Reaching across others is invasive and risks knocking things over.

Slide 8: 
Content: Utensil Mastery — How to Hold & Use Cutlery | 🇺🇸 American Style | Cut: Fork in left, knife in right. After cutting, switch fork to right hand to eat. Hold: Index finger on the spine of the knife, not wrapped around the handle. Rest (still eating): Fork (tines up) and knife in an inverted 'V' or crossed on plate. Finished: Both utensils parallel, at 4 o'clock position, tines up. | 🇬🇧 Continental/European Style | Cut & eat without switching : Fork stays in left hand (tines down), knife in right throughout. Manoeuvre food: Use knife to guide food onto the back of the fork. Rest (still eating): Fork tines down, knife and fork in an X on the plate. Finished: Both utensils parallel, tines down, handles at 4 o'clock. | 💡 Both styles are acceptable — choose one and be consistent throughout the meal.

Slide 9: 
Content: Eating Technique & Pace | 1 | Wait until everyone is served | Don't begin eating until the host starts or signals everyone to begin. In formal settings, wait for the host to pick up their fork. | 2 | Small bites only | Cut food into bite-sized pieces one at a time — don't cut the whole portion at once (American exception for children). Eat slowly and deliberately. | 3 | Chew before talking | Never talk with food in your mouth. Take small bites that allow you to speak when needed. A good meal is also a good conversation. | 4 | Don't slurp or blow on hot food | Wait for it to cool. Tilting your soup bowl away from you (to spoon the last drops) is acceptable at formal settings. | 5 | Bread: tear, don't cut | Break bread into small pieces before buttering. Butter only the piece you're about to eat — don't butter the whole slice. | 6 | Pace yourself with others | Try to eat at a similar pace to your tablemates. Finishing first or last can make others feel uncomfortable.

Content: Table Talk — The Art of Dining Conversation | ✅ GREAT Topics | Hobbies and shared interests Travel experiences and aspirations Books, films, music, podcasts School projects or achievements Local events and community news Fun 'what would you rather' questions Complimenting the food or setting | ❌ AVOID These Topics | Money, salaries, or costs of items Controversial politics or religion (unless close family) Other people's appearance or weight Gross medical or bodily topics Complaining about the food or host Gossip or talking about absent people Arguments or confrontational debate | 💡 When in doubt — ask questions and be a great listener. People love talking about themselves!

Slide 11: 
Content: 📱 Phone-Free Tables — Digital Dining Etiquette (2025) | 📵 | The Phone Stack Game | A growing trend — everyone stacks their phones face-down in the center. First to check theirs pays or does the dishes! | 🔕 | Silence & Pocket Rule | Phone on silent and kept away from the table — bag, pocket, or purse. Not on the table, even face-down. | 📸 | Food Photography Etiquette | If you must photograph food, do so quickly and discreetly before eating. No elaborate setups or multiple shots while others wait. | 🚨 | Genuine Emergencies Only | If expecting a critical call, inform your tablemates at the start. Excuse yourself from the table to take calls. | 👀 | No Scrolling Under the Table | People notice. It signals boredom and disrespect. If you're bored, engage — ask someone a question. | 💬 | Group Chats Can Wait | Responding to group messages during a meal is avoidable. The people in front of you deserve your full attention.

Slide 12: 
Content: PART ONE | Informal Dining | Restaurants, Friends' Homes & Casual Meals | Relaxed doesn't mean rules-free — it means being gracious without rigidity

Slide 13: 
Content: Casual Restaurant Manners | 🚶 | Wait to be seated | Even at casual spots, wait for a host to seat you. Don't just pick any table or wander around. | 📋 | Read the menu yourself | Don't ask the server to explain every item. If something is unfamiliar, search discreetly or ask one specific question. | 🙌 | Treat servers with respect | Servers are professionals. Say please, thank you, make eye contact, and don't snap fingers or wave to get attention. | Stacking plates myth | It's helpful at home, but in restaurants avoid stacking dirty plates — it's the server's job and can be seen as presumptuous. | 💳 | Offer to pay or split fairly | When dining with friends, discuss splitting before ordering. If someone treated you, acknowledge it and offer to treat next time. | ⭐ | Leave appropriate tips | Standard in many countries is 10-20% for good service. Learn the local custom wherever you dine.

Slide 14: 
Content: Dining at Someone's Home | Arrive on time — not early (disruptive) or more than 10 min late (inconsiderate) | Bring something: a dessert, flowers, or a small gift for the host — it's a thoughtful gesture | Compliment the food even if it isn't your favourite — acknowledge the effort | Eat what's served. Avoid saying 'I don't like this' — try a polite small portion | Offer to help clear or set up but don't insist if declined | Ask before using facilities and be respectful of their home | Thank the host personally before leaving — a follow-up text the same day is a bonus | Never arrive hungry and rush the meal — it's about fellowship, not just food

Slide 15: 
Content: Fast Food & Casual Eateries — Yes, Manners Still Matter | 🗑️ | Clean up after yourself | Dispose of wrappers, trays, and cups. Don't leave messes for staff. Most fast food is self-service for a reason. | 📢 | Indoor voice always | Loud laughing, shouting, or blasting music from your phone affects other patrons. Be mindful of shared public spaces. | 💺 | Don't hog tables | Once finished, leave promptly, especially during busy hours. Don't camp at a table when others are waiting. | 🤝 | Queue properly | Wait your turn, don't cut in line, and don't hover over people who haven't finished ordering yet. | 🍟 | Sharing etiquette | Offer before stealing fries! Ask before taking food off someone else's tray. It's about consent even in casual settings. | 🚿 | Hygiene first | Wash hands before eating. Use napkins. Don't lick fingers and then handle shared condiment dispensers.

Slide 16: 
Content: PART TWO | Formal Dining | Fine Dining, Banquets & Special Occasions | Where confidence meets occasion — navigate any formal table with grace

Slide 17: 
Content: Arriving, Seating & First Impressions at Formal Events | 🕐 | Arrive 5–10 minutes early | Formal events start on time. Arriving late disrupts seating, service, and the host's plan. Never arrive early either. | 👗 | Dress appropriately | Check the dress code in advance: Black Tie, Smart Casual, Business Formal. When in doubt, dress slightly more formally. | 🪑 | Don't seat yourself | Wait for the host, usher, or escort card to guide where you sit. Assigned seating is deliberate. | 🤵 | Let ladies and seniors sit first | Hold a chair for others and wait until the most senior guest is seated before sitting yourself. | 👜 | Bag & coat protocol | Bags go on the floor — never on the table or the empty chair beside you. Coats go to the cloakroom. | 📇 | Table cards & escort cards | Escort cards at the door show your table number. Table cards at the place setting show your exact seat. Follow both.

Slide 18: 
Content: Navigating a Multi-Course Formal Meal | 1 | Amuse-Bouche | A bite-sized intro from the chef. Eat in one bite. No sharing or cutting. | 2 | Appetiser / Starter | Pace yourself — don't finish too fast. Use outermost fork. Bread may be served now. | 3 | Soup | Spoon away from you. Tilt bowl away. Sip from side of spoon — don't slurp. | 4 | Fish Course | Use the fish fork and knife (if separate). Set aside any garnish not intended to eat. | 5 | Sorbet / Intermezzo | A palate cleanser between courses. Eat slowly with the small spoon provided. | 6 | Main / Entrée | The centrepiece of the meal. Take time, enjoy, and don't rush. Match pace with others. | 7 | Cheese Board | Take 2–3 small pieces. Use the provided knife. Don't touch multiple cheeses with the same knife. | 8 | Dessert & Coffee | Use the dessert spoon or fork above your plate. Coffee is typically the very last item served.

Slide 19: 
Content: Beverage Etiquette at Formal Tables | 🥂 | Toasting Protocol | Stand if others do. Hold your glass at the stem (not the bowl). Maintain eye contact when clinking. Sip after the toast — don't drain the glass. | 🍷 | Wine Glasses | Hold red wine glasses by the bowl (to warm), white and rosé by the stem (to keep cool). Never fill more than halfway. | 💧 | Water Comes First | Your water glass is typically top right. Sip water throughout the meal to cleanse your palate between courses. | 🫖 | Tea & Coffee | Place spoon on saucer after stirring — don't leave it in the cup. Don't slurp. Milk before or after tea is a personal choice — both are fine. | 🚫 | Underage? No Problem | Politely decline alcohol with 'No thank you' — no lengthy explanation needed. Request sparkling water or juice as an elegant alternative. | 🫗 | Refill Etiquette | Don't refill your own glass at a hosted dinner — wait for staff. If at a casual dinner, pour for others before yourself.

Slide 20: 
Content: Handling Awkward Situations Gracefully | 🤢 Food you hate lands on your plate? | Take a tiny portion, move it around, and don't make a scene. A polite 'I'll try everything' usually suffices. | 🍴 Dropped a utensil? | Quietly ask for a replacement. Don't pick it up off the floor and continue using it. | 😬 Food stuck in your teeth? | Excuse yourself to the restroom discreetly. Never pick your teeth at the table. | 🤧 Need to cough or sneeze? | Cover with your elbow, not your hands. Excuse yourself briefly. A handkerchief is ideal at formal settings. | 💸 Can't afford something on the menu? | Diplomatically choose a mid-range item. It's not appropriate to ask the host about prices — choose based on the occasion. | 🍽️ Spilled something? | Calmly blot with your napkin — don't rub. Alert the server quietly. Apologise briefly and move on without over-dramatising.

Slide 21: 
Content: Global Dining — Cultural Differences Teens Should Know | 🇯🇵 Japan | Slurping noodles is a compliment. Say 'Itadakimasu' before eating. Never stick chopsticks upright in rice. | 🇨🇳 China | Leaving food on your plate shows the host gave enough. Banging chopsticks is rude. Pouring tea for elders first is respectful. | 🇮🇳 India | Eat with your right hand only in traditional settings. Leaving a little food shows you're full. Remove shoes before entering a home. | 🇫🇷 France | Meals are long, multi-course affairs. Never ask for a doggy bag. Bread is for eating, not for soaking sauce until others do. | 🇸🇦 Middle East | Accept all food offered — refusing is impolite. Eat with right hand. Never eat before the host signals. | 🇲🇽 Mexico | Dinner is often late (8–10 PM). Bringing a gift for the host is expected. Never turn down a dish without tasting.

Slide 25: 
Content: Dining in 2025 — Modern Trends & Evolving Norms | 🌱 | Sustainability Awareness | It's now polite to acknowledge and appreciate restaurants' sustainability practices. Being mindful of food waste is part of modern etiquette. | 🌍 | Dietary Inclusivity | In 2025, hosts are expected to cater to dietary needs. Thank the chef briefly if they visit your table — eye contact and a genuine smile suffice.
